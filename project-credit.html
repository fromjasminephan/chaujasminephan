Business problem

A warehouse-style retailer needed a clearer picture of its sales landscape — which regions drove the most revenue, which brands were actually performing, and which products were silently dragging down inventory efficiency. The raw dataset required significant feature engineering before any of that was possible, with the ultimate goal of delivering data-driven recommendations on which items to cut.

What I built

  • Engineered three new features — BRAND, CATEGORY, and Owner — extracted from raw item description text
  • Built a correlation heatmap and time-series trend analysis in Python (pandas, matplotlib)
  • Ran chi-square tests + Cramér's V to assess brand-geography associations across 5 geographic variables
  • Designed a multi-factor inventory removal scoring model in Tableau (return rate, unit price, sales quantity)
  • Delivered a dual-axis visualization identifying the top 5 underperforming items for inventory review

Analytical approach

  • Standardized date fields and data types; introduced BRAND, CATEGORY, Owner as enrichment columns
  • Filtered recalled items and return-only records to ensure clean brand-level comparisons
  • Correlation heatmap across 9 features — confirmed sales qty ↔ revenue (r = 0.79)
  • Chi-square test across STATE, REGION, CITY, DISTRICT, LCNAME — all p = 0.000; Cramér's V ≤ 0.065
  • Removal score = weighted penalty for high return rate (20–50% band), low unit price, and low sales volume
  • Scoped removal analysis to HP, DELL, LENOVO to preserve overall brand diversity

Key findings

  • November 2018 sales spiked ~3.7× above baseline — driven by Black Friday and holiday promotions
  • EC region generated $200M+, more than all other regions combined
  • HP, DELL, LENOVO accounted for 96% of total sales with low refund rates of 13.5–15.0%
  • ASUS and ACER showed 20%+ refund rates despite low volume — signaling quality or expectation mismatches
  • Brand-geography correlation is statistically significant but practically negligible (Cramér's V < 0.07)
  • Some items showed >100% return rates — a data timing artifact flagged as a methodology caveat

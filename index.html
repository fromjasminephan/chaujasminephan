WEVOLV Salary Benchmarking System

Northeastern University ALY6980 Capstone  ·  Sponsor: WEVOLV  ·  2026
7,977contract records
R² 0.839Ridge & Lasso
60cohort cells

The problem

International basketball players outside the NBA negotiate contracts with no reliable salary benchmarks, creating structural information asymmetry that enables chronic underpayment. WEVOLV — an AI platform for athlete empowerment — needed an analytics engine to answer: "For someone like you, where does this offer fall relative to the market?"

My contribution

I led Ridge and Lasso regression modeling, Lasso coefficient analysis, and built the cohort quantile engine that powers the dashboard's empirical percentile output. My engine computes P25/P50/P75 salary within 60 peer groups (3 tiers × 5 positions × 4 experience brackets) and implements a tiered broadening fallback for sparse cohorts. I also discovered that 50.9% of raw user accounts were duplicate player profiles — a data governance finding delivered directly to WEVOLV and resolved using a composite identity key (name + date of birth + season).

Key findings

Market tier membership accounts for 88.1% of model gain, meaning the cohort quantile engine — not the predictive model — is the core product. Lasso coefficient analysis confirmed every measured feature carries independent salary signal. The fair range intersection logic (combining ML prediction bands with empirical P25–P75) classified 48.9% of contracts as FAIR, 25.7% as UNDERPAID, and 25.4% as OVERPAID across all 7,977 records.

Salary Transparency Index — International Basketball

Northeastern University ALY6980 Individual Capstone  ·  2026
7,867salary records
R² 0.728Random Forest
8leagues scored

The problem

International basketball players negotiate contracts in markets with wildly uneven information access. The NBA publishes every contract publicly; the Chinese Basketball Association publishes almost none. This opacity enables chronic underpayment, especially for players from developing economies with no agent representation and no access to salary benchmarks.

What I built

I designed a 4-dimension Salary Transparency Index (database availability 40%, media reporting 30%, policy accessibility 20%, financial disclosure 10%) and scored 8 major international leagues. The NBA scored 99.7/100; the CBA scored 3.5/100 — a 96-point gap. I then trained a Random Forest model on 7,867 records to quantify salary determinants and nationality effects, finding position accounts for 61.6% of variance and nationality for 2.8%. Phase 3 translated findings into a market opportunity matrix, identifying the EuroLeague and CBA as a combined $5.6M annual revenue opportunity for WEVOLV.

Key findings

Leagues with formal player associations score approximately 51 points higher on transparency (t = 4.35, p = .005). Revenue correlates strongly with transparency (r = .80, p = .017). Nationality contributes only 2.8% of salary variance once position and experience are controlled — the perceived US/non-US gap is a tier-distribution artifact, not direct discrimination.

German Bank Credit Risk & Customer Segmentation

Northeastern University ALY6080  ·  Professor Bedayat  ·  2025
1,000applicant records
3customer segments
79%SVM accuracy

The problem

Banks face a core challenge evaluating loan applicants with no prior credit history — standard scoring models fail entirely. This project asked: can behavioral and demographic features alone predict default risk, and how can customers be segmented to enable tailored lending strategies?

What we built

We applied K-means clustering (k=3, validated via elbow method and silhouette score of 0.212) to segment 1,000 German bank customers into three distinct borrower profiles. PCA retained 90% of variance for visualization with no impact on cluster quality. We engineered a three-tier risk label (Low/Medium/High) based on financial indicators, then trained an SVM classifier with a linear kernel to predict default risk — achieving 79% overall accuracy and strong F1 scores for Low (0.80) and Medium (0.81) risk classes. Switching from RBF to linear kernel improved accuracy from 49% to 79%.

Key findings

Three borrower profiles emerged: asset-heavy mature borrowers (avg age 42, avg loan 6,342 DM), young independent starters (avg age 32, smaller shorter-term loans), and balanced mid-age homeowners (avg age 36, moderate cross-category spending). Savings and checking account status showed the strongest negative correlation with default risk, while job type showed virtually no correlation once other features were controlled.

Predicting National Happiness — World Happiness Report

Northeastern University ALY-6040  ·  Professor O'Farrell  ·  2025
3country clusters
0.575best AUC (RF)
4models compared

The problem

What socioeconomic factors most significantly predict national happiness scores, and how can governments prioritize interventions to improve citizen wellbeing? This project applied a full data mining pipeline to the World Happiness Report dataset to answer that question.

What I built

The project ran in four phases. Phase 1 applied K-means clustering (k=3 via elbow method) to segment countries into three tiers: developing nations (low GDP, low happiness), emerging economies (medium GDP), and developed nations (high GDP, high happiness). Phase 2 fit a linear regression that revealed poor fit and non-linearity in the happiness relationship. Phase 3 trained a Random Forest identifying Unemployment Rate, Internet Access, and Public Health Index as top predictors. Phase 4 applied XGBoost, which surprisingly ranked Political Stability first (12.57% gain) above GDP per Capita.

Key findings

No single variable explains national happiness — the relationship is multidimensional and non-linear. All models showed limited predictive power (best AUC 0.575), highlighting fundamental data limitations rather than model failure. Internet Access appeared in both the RF and XGBoost top 5 features, suggesting digital infrastructure is an underappreciated policy lever. A post-2020 dip was visible across all clusters, consistent with COVID-19 disruption.

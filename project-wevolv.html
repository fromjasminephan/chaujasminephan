Business problem

International basketball players outside the NBA negotiate contracts with no reliable salary benchmarks. Teams hold all the information; athletes have none. This structural asymmetry enables chronic underpayment. WEVOLV — an AI platform for athlete empowerment — needed a data engine to answer: "For someone like you, where does this offer fall relative to the market?"

I designed and built the full salary benchmarking system: Ridge and Lasso regression with 5-fold cross-validation, a cohort quantile engine computing P25/P50/P75 across 60 peer groups (3 tiers × 5 positions × 4 experience brackets), and the fair range intersection logic combining ML prediction bands with empirical distributions. Along the way, I uncovered that 50.9% of user accounts were duplicate profiles — flagged and delivered as a data governance finding to WEVOLV.

Analytical approach

  • Extracted data from PostgreSQL (AWS RDS) via SQLAlchemy — 47,415 raw records
  • Standardized 20 position formats, converted 16 currencies to USD
  • Deduplication using composite identity key (name + DOB + season)
  • Ridge: best possible linear prediction under multicollinearity
  • Lasso: feature elimination to identify data collection priorities
  • Fair range = intersection of ML 80% prediction band and empirical P25–P75

Key findings

  • Market tier accounts for 88.1% of model gain — tier is the product, not prediction
  • Lasso retained all 8 features at best alpha (0.0001) — no redundant variables
  • Fair range engine classified 48.9% FAIR, 25.7% UNDERPAID, 25.4% OVERPAID
  • Nationality contributes only 0.5% of model gain — the US/non-US gap is a tier artifact
  • Players aged 29–31 are most systematically underpaid across all tiers
